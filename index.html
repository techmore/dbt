Start Here

This site is for beginners. It gives you a repeatable routine, simple choices when you do not know what you feel, and a short list of strong DBT resources to keep you focused. This is not a diagnosis or treatment replacement. If you are in crisis, call emergency services or 988 in the US immediately.

How to use this today: start with the next action, then use the morning, midday, and nighttime scaffold in order if you have time.

The body check is a mindfulness practice. The daily goal is not one fixed number of minutes; it is to complete the smallest useful practice block you can do consistently, then expand from there.

What this site gives you

  • A step-by-step 0-to-1 starting protocol
  • Daily guidance that fits in 20–35 minutes
  • Option-based forms when you cannot name feelings clearly
  • A resource tab for reinforcement and review

What to use it with

  • Sleep, food, water, movement
  • Short phone-free practice blocks
  • A coach or therapist, if available
  • One skill at a time, repeated daily

From 0 to 1: the first 14 days

Day 1–2: Stabilize

Only two habits:

  1. Check your body every 3 hours: jaw, shoulders, breathing, hands, stomach, feet
  2. Use the diary card first; if you cannot name what you feel, use the selector sheet

Body-check means: “Where in my body do I feel tension right now?”

Day 3–5: Build observation

Add one core skill:

  • Mindfulness of current emotions before reacting
  • Say: “I am having the emotion, not being the emotion.”

Day 6–10: Add regulation loop

Add one distress tolerance skill:

  • TIPP (cold water, brief movement, paced breathing)
  • Use on first sign of escalation, then continue the routine

Day 11–14: Build interpersonal capacity

Keep existing habits and add one communication target:

  • Use one DEAR skill sentence during a short real-life interaction
  • Keep interactions brief and specific to one objective

What Success Looks Like

DBT progress is measured by repetition and skill use, not by perfection.

Daily

  • You completed at least one practice block.
  • You used one skill on purpose.
  • You recorded the day in the diary card, or used the selector sheet if needed.

Weekly

  • You practiced on 5 of 7 days.
  • You reviewed one chain or difficult moment.
  • You adjusted the next week using evidence, not mood.

Monthly

  • You recover faster from stress than when you started.
  • You can identify the right skill sooner.
  • You have fewer full-spiral, no-action days.

Daily Schedule

Keep it short. Repeat the same structure until it becomes automatic. A good baseline is 10 to 20 minutes total; 20 to 35 minutes is the fuller version.

First move: start with the next action for today, then use the morning, midday, and nighttime blocks as the scaffold.

Body check

Mindfulness practice: notice tension, breathing speed, jaw, shoulders, hands, stomach, feet. Success looks like naming one body cue before reacting.

Paced breathing

Slow, steady breathing on a count. A simple version is 4 in, 6 out. Success looks like slowing your breathing for 2-3 minutes without forcing it.

Activated

Meaning: your body and mind are ramped up, irritated, panicked, flooded, shut down, or close to doing something impulsive. Success looks like noticing it early enough to use a skill.

Self-soothing

Meaning: a non-harmful action that calms your senses or body, such as cold water, a walk, a warm drink, music, texture, or slow breathing. Success looks like choosing one action and finishing it.

Sleep timing

If bedtime is 9:30 pm and your device gives you a 30 to 45 minute wind-down, the wind-down block starts around 8:45 to 9:00 pm. The goal is to reduce stimulation before bed, not to do more work.

  • Before wind-down: finish the evening review, stop task switching, and handle anything essential only.
  • Wind-down: dim lights, lower phone use, do one calm routine, and keep the same sequence nightly.
  • During wind-down: no problem-solving, no doom scrolling, no new projects.
  • Night is complete when: the diary card is done, the phone is down, and lights-out begins.

Morning (7–12 min)

  • 2-minute body check
  • Choose one focus word: calm, steady, clear, patient, or firm
  • 3 minutes of paced breathing (4-6 count cycle)
  • 10-second values statement: “Today I will prioritize one useful action.” Success looks like starting the day without overcomplicating it.
  • Checkpoint: write down what you notice right now, what you need, and the one next action after the shower.

Midday (8–12 min)

  • Use STOP if you notice stress building or you are about to react fast
  • STOP = Stop, Take a step back, Observe, Proceed mindfully
  • Choose one self-soothing action from the list below. Success looks like interrupting autopilot and doing one calm action before the next response.

Evening (8–15 min)

  • Fill out the diary card; use the selector sheet only if you cannot name what you feel
  • Write one alternative response for tomorrow. Success looks like a clear, specific next step.
  • Choose tomorrow’s one-skill focus. Success looks like picking one skill only, not five.

When something goes wrong

  • Use the diary card for a brief factual check-in.
  • Then do one chain analysis on the hardest moment.
  • Success looks like finding one place to interrupt the next chain earlier.

DBT Skill Set

DBT is usually taught as four core modules. This page keeps them visible and practical.

Mindfulness

Core goal: increase awareness before action.

  • Observe, Describe, Participate
  • One thing at a time during one task
  • Success looks like noticing what is happening without immediately acting on it

Emotion Regulation

Core goal: reduce emotional volatility.

  • Check current emotions for intensity, duration, context
  • Build one opposite action and do it consistently
  • Success looks like fewer emotional surprises and faster recovery

Distress Tolerance

Core goal: survive urges without harm.

  • TIPP protocol at first wave of overwhelm
  • Self-soothing by senses: cold, texture, scent
  • Success looks like getting through the urge without making the moment worse

Interpersonal Effectiveness

Core goal: keep your values and boundaries in conversation.

  • DEAR: Describe, Express, Assert, Reinforce
  • One objective, one sentence, one request
  • Success looks like making a clear request without escalating the situation

Chain Analysis

Core goal: understand sequence and build alternatives. Start with the event, then work backward and forward.

  • Prompting event: what happened right before the problem.
  • Vulnerability: sleep, food, stress, substance use, pain, conflict, or other factors that lowered your margin.
  • Chain: the thoughts, body sensations, emotions, and actions that followed.
  • Consequence: what happened right after and later.
  • Repair / future step: where one different action could fit next time.

Weekly Structure (Not Overwhelming)

Week 1

Use only one skill per day and one daily checkpoint. Keep sleep baseline stable. Fill out the diary card at least once.

Week 2

Add evening tracking and one interpersonal target on 2 days. Review one chain analysis.

Week 3

Keep previous habits and add one interpersonal communication script on 3 days. Use the diary card daily if possible. Aim for stability, not intensity.

Week 4+

Repeat with one refinement each week: slower pace, clearer boundaries, better recovery speed. Compare current week to last week using the progress calendar.
